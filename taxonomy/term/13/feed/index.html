https://www.ipcuk.events/presentation/Soil-Seeds-Social-Change-El-Salvador <div class="field field-name-title field-type-ds field-label-hidden"><div class="field-items"><div class="field-item even"><h2>Soil, Seeds &amp; Social Change – Learning from the Permaculture Movement of El Salvador</h2></div></div></div><div class="field field-name-body field-type-text-with-summary field-label-hidden"><div class="field-items"><div class="field-item even"><p><span style="font-size:16px">The permaculture movement of El Salvador has been growing and changing for just over fifteen years, against a backdrop of post-war economic instability, gang violence, and gender inequalities. In some ways, Salvadorian permaculture shows us an image of an ‘alter-globalisation’ – a vision of a world connected by forms of sharing and exchange that strengthen traditional practices and value local resources, as opposed to markets and calculable profits. </span></p> <p><span style="font-size:16px">Permaculture design techniques arrived in El Salvador from the UK and from Australia, but found deep resonance with agroecological networks that were already growing there, as with visions of la Madre Tierra (Mother Nature) that were still present within indigenous heritage and practices. On the other hand, many challenges lie ahead for the movement, which is in a process of growth and change. Institutional frameworks systematically devalue the practical expertise of campesino farmers, and even the NGO culture of El Salvador is pervaded by elitism, factional loyalties, and machismo cultures.</span></p> <p><span style="font-size:16px">This session brings together three women who have different connections with the movement: Reina Mejia has been co-ordinating the movement for the past 8 years and works to build broad alliances for food sovereignty in El Salvador. Karen Inwood helped establish the Permaculture Institute of El Salvador and was its Director for 10 years, supporting the emergence of  a wider grassroots movement. Naomi Millner, a geographer, has been working with both women since December 2012 as a researcher, seeking to document the movement in terms of a broader politics of knowledge. </span></p> <p><span style="font-size:16px">In this session all three women reflect on the movement’s successes and challenges in its aspirations to enable and inspire people in poor rural communities to live and produce their food in harmony with their natural environment. Drawing on experience and on a short film they will speak to the question of ‘what works’ to motivate and maintain changes in attitude and culture, and what still remains in the way.</span></p>
